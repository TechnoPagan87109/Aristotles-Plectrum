# Governance

## Status 🟡 v1 In Progress

## Purpose

This document describes how authority and responsibility are distributed within the Community and the Corporation.

## Governance Overview

First and foremost, the Community is governed by its Members.

Software systems may assist governance in an ever-growing number of ways, but authority and responsibility remain with the Members. Technology may help Members understand complex issues, evaluate proposals, and coordinate activities, but it does not replace human judgment, responsibility, or accountability.

## Membership

The qualifications for Membership are determined by the Membership itself.

Requirements may include any combination of equity, experience, land, labor, expertise, or other contributions considered valuable to the Community.

As the Community evolves, Membership requirements may evolve as well.

Members are simultaneously:

* Owners of the Corporation,
* Participants in governance,
* Residents of the Community,
* Employees of the Corporation,
* and volunteer participants in Community research and experimentation.

Memberships are equal.

### Source of Authority

The authority of the Community and Corporation originates from the Membership.

Technology, infrastructure, management systems, and governance mechanisms exist to assist the Members in exercising that authority, not to replace it.

Each Member has an equal voice in Community governance.

Each Member is associated with a Membership token representing their participation in Community governance.

These tokens are used to verify Membership and support governance processes. Because each Member receives one Membership token, authority remains distributed equally among the Members.

Membership tokens represent participation in Community governance, not ownership of Community assets.

## The Corporation

The Corporation serves as the legal and economic framework that supports the Community.

The Corporation owns and operates Community infrastructure, participates in the external economy, and provides the resources required to maintain and expand Community capabilities.

The Members govern both the Community and the Corporation.

The Corporation is a Member-owned, not-for-profit organization.

There are no external shareholders receiving dividends.

Resources generated by the Corporation are used to:

* operate and maintain the Community,
* expand Community capabilities,
* support Community priorities,
* and provide resources for Member use.

Its primary purpose is to develop and maintain the hardware, software, and culture necessary to support an emulation of Post-Scarcity.

## Distributed Ledger Governance

Distributed ledger technologies may be used to provide transparent and auditable records of:

* Membership,
* decisions,
* votes,
* agreements,
* resource allocations,
* and smart contracts.

These systems support governance by providing transparency and accountability, but they do not replace the authority of the Members.

The Community is operated through a combination of:

* deliberative decision-making,
* AI-assisted analysis,
* and transparent record-keeping systems.

### Internal and External Operations

The Corporation operates in two different environments.

Externally, it participates in the existing market economy and follows applicable laws, regulations, and business practices.

Internally, it operates using systems designed to support an emulation of Post-Scarcity.

These internal and external systems serve different purposes and should not be confused.

## Governance Mechanisms

The Community draws ideas from several governance traditions, including:

* Deliberative Democracy

Members exchange information, viewpoints, priorities, and concerns in order to improve proposals and develop broadly acceptable solutions.

Historically, deliberative processes have often been limited by the amount of time and effort required to review information and participate effectively.

([AI-Assisted Decision Support](../software/ai-assisted.md) AI and machine learning tools can help identify patterns, hidden assumptions, areas of agreement, and sources of disagreement within large amounts of information. This can significantly accelerate the iterative and recursive process of proposal refinement.

These are discussed in greater detail in separate documents.
