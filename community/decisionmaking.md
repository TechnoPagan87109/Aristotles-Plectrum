# Decision Making

## Status 🟢 Draft

## Purpose

This document describes how proposals are created, evaluated, refined, and adopted within the Community.

The objective of the process is not to find perfect solutions. The objective is to help Members identify problems, explore alternatives, understand trade-offs, and develop solutions that are broadly acceptable to the Community.

---

## Overview

The Community uses a deliberative decision-making process.

Members propose ideas, evaluate proposals, identify concerns, suggest improvements, and ultimately decide whether a proposal should be adopted.

The process is designed to improve proposals over time rather than immediately accepting or rejecting them.

AI-assisted tools may help Members understand proposals and identify opportunities for improvement, but authority remains with the Members.

---

## Problem Identification

Decision-making begins with identifying a problem, opportunity, or goal.

Examples may include:

* expanding food production,
* reducing energy consumption,
* purchasing new equipment,
* changing Community policies,
* or modifying existing processes.

Before a proposal can proceed, Members should be able to identify the problem being addressed and provide evidence that the problem exists.

The Community attempts to distinguish between actual conditions and perceived conditions whenever practical.

---

## Proposal Creation

Any Member may submit a proposal.

A proposal should clearly describe:

* the problem being addressed,
* the proposed solution,
* expected benefits,
* expected costs,
* known risks,
* and how success will be measured.

Where practical, proposals should identify any resources, infrastructure, or Community commitments required for implementation.

---

## Initial Review

Before broader deliberation begins, proposals may be reviewed for:

* legal compliance,
* regulatory requirements,
* conflicts with existing Community agreements,
* resource availability,
* and technical feasibility.

The purpose of this review is not to approve or reject proposals, but to identify constraints that may affect implementation.

---

## Community Deliberation

Members review proposals through the subsystems of the Community Operating System.

Members may:

* discuss proposals,
* ask questions,
* identify concerns,
* propose alternatives,
* and suggest revisions.

The goal of deliberation is not debate for its own sake.

The goal is to improve understanding and improve the quality of the proposal.


---

## AI-Assisted Analysis

AI-assisted tools may be used to help Members evaluate proposals.

These tools may:

* identify conflicts and trade-offs,
* highlight hidden assumptions,
* identify duplicate or overlapping proposals,
* explore possible consequences,
* suggest revisions,
* and summarize discussions.

These analyses are not predictions of the future.

Their purpose is to help Members better understand the proposal and its possible effects.

---

## Satisfaction Ratings

Members may evaluate proposals using satisfaction ratings.

Ratings may be applied to:

* the proposal as a whole,
* individual sections,
* specific resource allocations,
* or proposed implementation methods.

Members may also provide comments explaining their ratings.

The purpose of these ratings is to identify areas of agreement, disagreement, and opportunities for improvement.

---

## Revision and Refinement

Member feedback is collected and analyzed.

AI-assisted systems may identify:

* common concerns,
* recurring suggestions,
* hidden conflicts,
* opportunities for compromise,
* and sections generating significant disagreement.

Based on this information, revised proposals may be generated.

These revised proposals are then returned to the Membership for further evaluation.

The process may repeat multiple times.

The objective is to increase understanding and satisfaction where possible.

Improvement is not guaranteed. Some disagreements may reflect genuine differences in priorities or values.

---

## Proposal Convergence

Over time, proposals may:

* gain support,
* be revised,
* merge with other proposals,
* split into multiple proposals,
* or be abandoned.

When a proposal reaches a sufficient level of support and stability, it may proceed to a final vote.

The exact thresholds are determined by the Membership and may evolve over time.

---

## Adoption

Once approved, a proposal becomes Community policy, procedure, or project.

Implementation may include:

* resource allocation,
* scheduling,
* creation of smart contracts,
* modification of Community systems,
* or assignment of responsibilities.

The adopted proposal becomes part of the Community's operational framework.

---

## Review and Measurement

Implementation does not end the decision-making process.

The Community attempts to measure whether adopted proposals achieve their intended goals.

Where practical, outcomes are compared against the original objectives of the proposal.

Successful approaches may be expanded.

Unsuccessful approaches may be modified, replaced, or discontinued.

---

## Continuous Improvement

The decision-making process itself is subject to review and improvement.

Members may propose changes to:

* voting systems,
* satisfaction metrics,
* deliberation processes,
* AI-assisted tools,
* and governance procedures.

The objective is not to create a perfect system.

The objective is to create a system capable of learning from experience and producing better decisions over time.
