# WHAT?

This project aims to develop a comprehensive blueprint for—and document the construction of—an [Open Source](hardware/opensource.md), replicable [ecovillage](hardware/ecovillage.md) The community is [Governed](community/governance.md), by an [AI-assist](software/ai-assist.md) supported, ["deliberative, direct democratic"](community/ system) and is designed to support an [emulation of post-scarcity](community/nearpostscarcity.md) conditions.

At a high level, this requires solving three interconnected problems:
- how we build and sustain the physical systems we depend on,
- how we organize and govern ourselves,
- and how we distribute the benefits of automation.

## Core Features

### Open Source

Open source hardware (OSH) and software (OSS) consistently deliver measurable cost savings and operational advantages. Academic and industry studies report typical hardware unit cost reductions of roughly 50–90% for scientific and lab devices compared with commercial equivalents, driven by local fabrication, lower component costs, and reusable designs. For software, survey-based research finds organizations commonly report TCO reductions in the range of ~20–50% versus proprietary alternatives, with savings depending on how much community support and internal expertise are used versus paid commercial support. European Commission scenario modeling additionally suggests that wider public‑sector OSS/OSH adoption could lower ICT procurement costs by single‑digit to low double‑digit percentages over a 5–10 year horizon, while generating indirect productivity and competitiveness benefits.

Beyond direct cost reductions, OSS/OSH brings strong strategic advantages: no vendor lock‑in and greater interoperability reduce future migration and licensing risks; transparent designs increase trust and security by making behavior auditable; and open collaboration accelerates innovation and iteration as communities and firms share improvements and fixes. These combined effects—not just lower upfront costs but easier local customization, repairability, and faster feature development—compound into larger long‑term value, especially when organizations actively participate in or leverage healthy open communities.

### Ecovillage

Open source hardware and software can boost local food security and light manufacturing by enabling affordable, customizable tools (automated irrigation controllers, open-design greenhouse systems, seed-planting robots, small-scale food processors) that communities can build, repair, and adapt quickly—cutting procurement costs, reducing downtime, and accelerating local innovation. Shared designs let farmers and makers iterate for local climates, integrate sensors and automation, and scale production with low capital outlay while avoiding vendor lock‑in.

With full resource accounting, open designs make it feasible to track every material, energy, water, and waste flow because schematics, control software, and data formats are transparent and interoperable. That transparency enables processes where outputs from one system (e.g., food waste, excess heat, graywater) are captured and routed as inputs to another (compost, biogas, heat recovery, irrigation), creating a circular, measurable loop. Combining OSS/OSH with standardized sensors and open data formats supports continuous optimization, auditability, and community-driven improvements that raise efficiency, reduce waste, and improve long‑term sustainability.

### CoHousing

Cohousing within an ecovillage blends private homes with shared common spaces to foster collaboration, reduce resource use, and strengthen social resilience. Private units give residents autonomy and personal retreat, while intentionally designed commons—kitchens, workshops, gardens, childcare, and meeting spaces—support shared meals, pooled tools, cooperative childcare, and coordinated maintenance, lowering per‑capita costs and material footprints. Shared facilities enable efficient systems (centralized heating, communal food production, bulk purchasing, repair cafés) and make collective decisions about land use, energy, and stewardship, improving long‑term sustainability.

The balance of private and public space is deliberate: enough private space preserves dignity and diversity of lifestyles; enough commons builds trust, mutual aid, and economies of scale. Design principles—clear thresholds between private and shared areas, staged privacy (semi‑public patios, shared courtyards), flexible multipurpose buildings, and governance rules for access and upkeep—ensure usability and minimize conflict. When combined with open‑design technologies and full resource accounting, cohousing in an ecovillage becomes a living laboratory: shared data and open plans let the community optimize energy, water, waste flows, and local food systems, creating a transparent, circular, and adaptable neighborhood that scales both social capital and ecological efficiency.

### Governance

First and foremost, [Governance](community/governance.md) of the Community is the responsibility of the Members. [AI and ML](ai-assisted.md) can assist, in ever expanding ways, but the operations of Governance are dependent of Members.

## The Corporation

The Corporation is a Member-owned, not-for-profit organization.

There are no external shareholders receiving dividends.

Resources generated by the Corporation are used to:

* operate and maintain the Community,
* expand Community capabilities,
* support Community priorities,
* and provide resources for Member use.

Its primary purpose is to develop and maintain the hardware, software, and culture necessary to support an emulation of Post-Scarcity.

## Membership

Members are simultaneously:

* Owners of the Corporation,
* Participants in governance,
* Residents of the Community,
* Employees of the Corporation,
* and volunteer participants in Community experimentation.

Memberships are equal.

Each Member has an equal voice in Community governance.

The Community draws ideas from several governance traditions, including:

* Deliberative Democracy
* Distributed Ledger Governance
* Cooperative Governance
* AI-Assist

Deliberative Democracy can be terribly slow, but due to the wide variety of Machine Language tools for extracting hidden information from massive amounts of text, AI-Assist can make this useful.

### Near Post-Scarcity

In a full, post-scarcity society, autonomous agents gather the tools, materials, energy to produce the goods and services that the society needs and virtually zero cost. Full post-scarcity is not yet feasible at community scale due to technological limitations, intellectual property constraints, and cost. This model instead creates a practical emulation—[“near post-scarcity”](community/nearpostscarcity.md)

The community focuses on meeting the broad needs of its members while producing as much as possible locally through automation and efficient design. The result is a more resilient and stable way of living that reduces dependence on external systems while remaining connected to them where necessary.

The first difference between full and near post-scarcity is a large part of the "autonomous agents" will be the Members, and the second difference is that many of the things they will need, they won't be able to produce for themselves, regardless, for time being. The Corporation will need to sell surplus production for the funds to buy those products.

# WHY?

This project is a response to a set of converging pressures that existing systems are not well designed to handle.

## Automation

Automation continues to reduce the need for human labor across a wide range of industries. While this increases productive capacity, it also creates a disconnect between employment and access to resources.

If fewer people are required to produce what society needs, the question becomes:

* how are those goods distributed,
* and how do people meaningfully participate in that system?

As production becomes more automated and centralized, individuals have less direct influence over the systems that sustain them, shifting the question toward who builds, controls, and benefits from those systems.

---

## Fragility

Modern systems are highly efficient but often fragile.

Food, energy, and manufactured goods are typically produced far from where they are consumed, relying on complex supply chains that are vulnerable to disruption.

When those systems fail or degrade, local communities often have limited ability to compensate.

---

## Incentives

Many existing systems are optimized for cost and profit rather than resilience, transparency, or long-term sustainability. Generally, this does not end well.

This can lead to:

* overproduction in some areas,
* scarcity in others,
* and systems that are difficult to adapt or understand at the local level.

---

## Why This Approach

This project explores an alternative:

* localizing production where practical,
* sharing infrastructure to reduce duplication,
* using automation to increase capacity,
* and coordinating decisions through transparent, participatory systems.

The goal is to create a model that:

* is more resilient,
* more understandable,
* more adaptable at the community level,
* and, ideally, contributes to the resilience of the larger systems it connects to.

---

## Economic Framing

The community operates within the broader economic systems that currently exist.

Internally, however, it is organized differently. Instead of relying solely on markets or centralized planning, it coordinates production and resource use through shared systems, local capacity, and participatory governance.

**Q: At its core, is it a Capitalist or Socialist system?**

**A: No.**

This does not align cleanly with traditional economic categories such as capitalism or socialism.

The Community is organized around shared infrastructure, local production, automation, resource coordination, and participatory governance. It shares characteristics with both systems, but is ultimately attempting something different.

---

## What This Is Not

This is not a utopia.

It does not assume perfect behavior, complete self-sufficiency, or the elimination of external systems.

It is not intended to replace broader society.

Instead, it is an experimental model for improving how communities:

* produce what they need,
* manage shared resources,
* and adapt to changing conditions.

# HOW?

The Community is built by combining several existing ideas with newer technologies into a single integrated system.

No individual component is particularly revolutionary.

The goal is not to invent an entirely new technology, governance system, or economic model.

The goal is to combine proven approaches into a system that is more resilient, more transparent, and better able to support human flourishing in an increasingly automated world.

## Local Production

Where practical, the Community produces goods and services close to where they are consumed.

This reduces dependence on long supply chains and allows Members to directly observe and improve the systems that support them.

Examples include:

* food production,
* energy generation,
* manufacturing,
* maintenance,
* and education.

The objective is not complete self-sufficiency.

The objective is to increase local capability and resilience.

## Resource Coordination

Shared resources must be allocated fairly and transparently.

The Community uses the [Commons Allocation Index](community/cai.md) to coordinate access to scarce resources and production capacity.

The goal is not to maximize accumulation.

The goal is to ensure that resources are used efficiently and remain available to the Community as a whole.

## The Tragedy of the Commons

The Tragedy of the Commons describes a situation where a shared resource is available to a group, but individual incentives encourage overuse of that resource. Expect problems.

In its simplest form, each person receives the full benefit of additional use while the cost of that use is distributed across the entire group (everyone in the group has unlimited use of that resource). Over time, this can lead to depletion of the shared resource even when nobody intends harm.

The Community attempts to avoid these conditions through transparency, measurement, and resource coordination.

Shared resources are not treated as unlimited. Resource usage, maintenance requirements, availability, and capacity are monitored through the Community Operating System. Different categories of resources may have different allocation rules.

For example:

* Production equipment may use reservation and scheduling systems.
* Shared land may have usage agreements and maintenance responsibilities.
* Energy and water systems may have allocation mechanisms during periods of scarcity.
* Community vehicles may have scheduling, maintenance, and usage tracking.

The purpose of these systems is not to restrict access unnecessarily. The purpose is to ensure that shared resources remain available, functional, and sustainable for the Community as a whole.

The Community recognizes that successful commons require more than shared ownership. They require clear expectations, transparency, accountability, and ongoing stewardship.

## Deliberative Governance

Members participate directly in decisions affecting the Community.

Proposals are discussed, evaluated, revised, and improved through a deliberative process.

AI-assisted tools help Members:

* understand proposals,
* identify trade-offs,
* surface hidden assumptions,
* and explore possible consequences.

The AI assists.

Members decide.

See:

* [Governance](community/governance.md)
* [Decision Making](community/decisionmaking.md)

## Transparency and Feedback

The Community attempts to measure the consequences of its decisions.

Energy production, food production, resource usage, maintenance requirements, and other operational systems are monitored where practical.

The purpose is not surveillance.

The purpose is learning.

Successful approaches can be expanded.

Unsuccessful approaches can be modified or abandoned.

---

## Continuous Improvement

The Community is designed as an ongoing experiment.

No part of the system is assumed to be perfect.

Processes, technologies, and governance structures are expected to evolve over time as Members gain experience and new tools become available.

The objective is not to create a final answer.

The objective is to create a framework capable of finding better answers.
